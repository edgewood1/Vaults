# Form & Matter: Pine Bark — The Withdrawn and the Sensual

*Form-matter v2 — focused piece on the withdrawn/sensual distinction.*

---

## Observation

I press my hand against your bark.

- Your dry and brittle plates overlap. Deep irregular ravines, slate gray or charcoal at the edges.
- Fissures deepen into ochre and rust-red.
- One snaps under my thumb.

I scratch the surface and the scent arrives: sharp, medicinal, catching at the back of my throat. I guess a resinous under layer.

I brush my sleeve against you and you rasp. I peel a flake away — a sharp dry crack, then a softer tearing as the fibers let go.

I stand back and look at you. What am I actually holding? What makes you you and not something else?

When I consider you, you are — a tree and its parts; physical resistance and a particular kind of shape; both at once; parts whose whole is greater than the sum; shaped resistance.

But were I to pull them apart, as a sort of thought experiment, what would happen?

---

## Level One: The Withdrawn

I start with what I cannot see or touch, but what I know is there — which is, perhaps, where every love begins.

We can speak of you in terms of form and matter. But before either of these, I want to sit with something stranger and more fundamental.

Beneath your bark, beneath your specific substance, there is what I can only call a counterspace — a metaphysical receptive field in a state of near nothingness, a hole in reality that possesses an appetite for physicality. Not emptiness in the ordinary sense, but a silent hunger for existence, a gravitational pull toward actualization. It is the deeper possibility underlying everything you are.

Primary matter is this counterspace in the act of receiving — drawing a form into itself, filling with a specific identity, unfolding as that form enters. Through this unfolding, what was a dimensionless depth becomes a physical object with width, height, weight. You, casting shade. You, resisting my hand. Without this counterspace, you would be a mere idea — the memory or concept of a tree, hovering without location. It is what ensures your materiality, what extends you into physical space, what anchors you in reality even as your outward shape transforms across seasons and years.

This depth can also release and receive anew. Your bark falls and decomposes slowly into soil. You could burn, becoming ash dispersing on the wind. In each case, primary matter releases one form and opens itself to another. The same underlying receptivity that was once you becomes earth. Nothing disappears; something is received anew. You are, in this sense, on loan from a deeper possibility.

Now — this near-nothingness at the base of things could be read as the abyss. The void that makes everything contingent, arbitrary, unsupported. If what holds you is itself without qualities, without identity, without permanence — then perhaps nothing holds you at all. Perhaps existence floats over nothing, and the vertigo you feel when you contemplate this depth is simply the truth of things, undecorated. This is what certain philosophers have insisted. I do not dismiss them.

But what I notice, standing here with my hand against your bark, is that this depth is not indifferent. It receives. It holds. It draws form into itself with something that resembles hunger, or longing — a pull toward actualization rather than a slide toward dissolution. The counterspace is not a negation but an openness. And there is a difference between a void that swallows and a depth that welcomes. Whether that difference is everything or nothing, I cannot say with certainty. But I find I cannot address you — cannot say *you* at all — without implicitly choosing the second.

Substantial form is the companion to this depth — the organizing principle that answers the question of what kind of thing you are. Not just a physical object, but a pine. And I begin to sense it not by looking directly, but by watching what you do. You grow rather than crystallize. Your branches reach for the sun rather than shrink from it. Your roots stabilize rather than scatter. You cannot fly, swim, or speak. In what you can and cannot do, you are bound to every other pine — the same nature, the same limits, the same long patient reach toward light.

Together, this form and this depth constitute your withdrawn aspect — the level of you that neither eye nor hand can reach directly. They govern everything: your persistence through weathering and damage, and your mortality. When you finally decompose, when substantial form departs, what remains is no longer you. It is something in the process of becoming otherwise. The counterspace reasserts its openness, ready to receive another.

This withdrawn aspect can be thought, even if it cannot be seen. Some philosophers have suggested it withdraws absolutely — that nothing, not even thought, ever fully grasps what a thing essentially is. I am not certain they are wrong.

But when I contemplate this underlying depth — this counterspace that holds you now and will one day release you — I become aware of your fragility, and something in me softens into gratitude. What stands before me one day will not. What stands as me one day will not. The counterspace is patient. It is in no hurry.

I keep looking.

## Level Two: The Sensual

When I hold your bark in my hand: a waxy matrix, dense lignified fibers, a viscous resin permeating everything.

If we were to compare this bark to any other bark in the universe, we would find you to be absolutely unique. We would find that you match to you and no other oak.

This potential for extreme differentiation — uniqueness — is the work of actual matter. It is what makes you unrepeatable, located, here, in this specific Carolina dirt rather than nowhere.

It bends form into something else — a kind of accidental form. And as matter already shaped into specific stuff, it carries its own place, its own history, its own accumulation of seasons.

Through the withdrawn, two pines share a nature; through the sensual, each exceeds that abstraction.

If prime matter is your capacity to exist as something, secondary matter is your capacity to exist as *this* — the vessel for your biography.

Accidental form: a plate two inches thick or paper-thin. Ash-gray or deep rust-red. Brittle from drought or slightly supple after rain. These are the qualities that vary across trees, across seasons, without touching what you fundamentally are. The accidents are real — they are everything my senses deliver — but they are also where you meet the world.

Consider the wind. It never meets your withdrawn layer — it only ever meets your expressions, your branches, your bark, these surfaces shaped by accidental form.

And the way they answer carries your whole history. The lean of your trunk. The depth of roots on your exposed side. The branches thickened by years of gusting. These are the accumulated responses of your accidental form to the world's demands — a biography written in lean and thickness and depth of root. The record of every meeting between your nature and what the world has asked of you.

This is, perhaps, the most intimate thing about you. Your withdrawn aspect shares with every other pine. But your sensual aspects are where you differ — where you become you, marked by every season you have survived, every storm, every long summer. A particular, unrepeatable history written in bark and root.

When I run my hand across your surface and feel that roughness, smell that resinous sharpness, I am touching this directly — secondary matter expressing itself through accidental form. You, arriving at your own surface. Offering yourself to be known.

---

## What I'm Left With

I peel another flake and hold it in my palm.

There are two levels here, and at each a pair. At the first level — withdrawn, inaccessible to the senses — prime matter and substantial form together answer the question: what kind of thing are you? At the second level — sensual, biographical, reachable by hand — secondary matter and accidental form answer: which one? this one.

In both pairs, matter is the receptive aspect — what receives, individuates, makes change possible. Form is the active one — what shapes, directs, actualizes. And the withdrawn and the sensual are not two separate realities but two depths of the same one. The permanent only through the passing. The hidden making itself known in the world.

You are what you are through your substantial form. This you — these exact plates, this exact gray, this particular lean toward the southern light, this whole unrepeatable life — through your secondary matter and the accidents it carries.

The moment substantial form commits to prime matter, you enter the cycle: becoming, being, ceasing. What I am holding is somewhere in the middle of that arc. You are still growing. You are still you. But you will not always be.

I set the flake down at the base of your trunk.
