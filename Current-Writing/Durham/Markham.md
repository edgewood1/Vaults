

# From Apple notes

The Ground Beneath Markham and Washington: A History in Layers

Chapter One

The Birth of Durham’s Station

In 1849, the North Carolina Railroad Company was building a line from Goldsboro all the way to Charlotte, buying up land as they went.  When they reached our area, they made an offer to purchase four acres from a local doctor named Bartlett S. Durham.  He surprised them by donating it instead.

The railroad built a stop on that land and named it Durham’s Station in his honor.  It stood at the corner of what is now Peabody Street and Corcoran Street.  Around it grew a cluster of wooden buildings, a general store, and a loading platform where local farmers could ship their goods.  A town took shape around all of it.

Spreading out from the tracks were several large farms.  To the north lay the Green, Mangum, and Rigsbee farms.  The Geer farm sat at the northeastern edge of the settlement.  Further northwest, the Strayhorn farm occupied what is now the Old North Durham neighborhood, and beyond that the land gave way to hilly pine forest.

**Tobacco**

At the close of the Civil War, the largest troop surrender in American history took place at Bennett Farm, seven miles west of Durham Station.  While the negotiations dragged on, Union soldiers raided the warehouse of local farmer John R. Green and helped themselves to his stock of smoking tobacco.

When they went home, they took a taste for it with them.  Orders started arriving from across the country.  Green refined his product, branded it “Genuine Durham Smoking Tobacco,” and chose a bull as his trademark, borrowing the image from a tin of English mustard.  Demand kept growing.  The forests and farmland that had surrounded the depot were slowly overtaken by warehouses, factories, and auction houses.  The town that a doctor’s generosity had conjured out of a pine ridge was becoming a city built on smoke.

**The Duke Belt Line**

By 1890, the Dukes had built the Durham Belt Line, a crescent-shaped rail spur that looped around the northern edge of downtown, connecting the factories, warehouses, and mills into a single freight network.  At its two ends, it tied together the two rail lines that served the city: the original NC Railroad running east to west along Pettigrew Street, and a north-south corridor coming down from Virginia.  Every facility along the crescent could load cars directly as the line passed, sending goods out in either direction.  The Belt Line was the circulatory system of a city that ran on tobacco, and it ran without stopping.

**Strayhorn**

When the Civil War ended, so did slavery.  The people the Strayhorn family had enslaved were free - but freedom without land or money left few real options.  Most stayed on, taking the Strayhorn family name and working the same land under tenant arrangements, the geography of their lives largely unchanged even as their legal status had been transformed.

As Durham’s tobacco industry expanded northward through the 1870s and 1880s, the Strayhorn farm was sold off piece by piece.  What remained was a narrow strip along what are now Dacian and Macon streets, between Washington and Glendale.  By 1890, roughly 65 people lived there, Black and white.  Most of the freedmen had found work in the tobacco factories, or as domestic workers and day laborers.  Beyond this settlement, the land fell away into marshy ground thick with scrub oak and standing water - the soft, unglamorous edge of a city that was growing too fast to look back at what it was leaving behind.

**How the Neighborhood Built Itself: South to North**

In 1892, Brodie Duke opened the Pearl Cotton Mills on Trinity Avenue, between Washington Street and Duke Street.  By 1895 it employed 200 workers running 10,000 spindles and 160 looms, producing fine muslin and extra-wide sheeting.  It would keep running until it was demolished in 1971.

Housing for workers spread along Washington, Orient, and Foster streets, eventually totaling 35 houses.  Workers who couldn’t get a company house found rentals nearby, including a row of one-story frame cottages along the 800 block of North Duke Street.

The village was designed as white-only.  Black workers were offered only the dirtiest jobs - unloading cotton bales, stoking boiler rooms.  Black women found almost no employment at all.  White workers got a company church, a company store, and a school.  The Strayhorn community, one block away, got none of those things.  What the two communities shared was considerable - the same poverty, the same industrial air, the same unpaved streets.  What the system ensured was that they would never act on it.

The mill dumped its spent dye water directly into the creek.  The whole neighborhood smelled like a laundromat from the hot, soapy discharge.  The color of the water shifted with the day’s production: deep indigo, sulfurous purple, bright red.  Residents called it the ditch.  The Strayhorn Creek to the northeast, fed by cold springs rather than mill waste, still ran clear.

**The Landscape at Washington and Markham**

North of the Strayhorn settlement and Pearl Mill village, the ground continued falling toward a marshy low point at the Washington and Markham intersection.  Several springs to the east fed small tributaries that converged into Strayhorn Creek, which cut through that same intersection before flowing northwest to join the Ellerbe Creek tributary that runs along what is now the Ellerbe Creek Trail.

That tributary gathered drainage from the surrounding ridges - Old North Durham, what is now Durham Central Park, the Durham School of the Arts, and Trinity Park - pooling at a confluence near the current South Ellerbe Restoration Project site at 808 West Trinity Avenue.  From there the water flowed toward Club Boulevard, then east to meet the main channel of Ellerbe Creek southeast of Northgate Park.  That main channel originates near Cole Mill Road, passes beneath the former Northgate Mall site, and continues northeast toward Falls Lake.

While the Washington and Markham intersection sat at the edge of this system, its ground was nevertheless softer and less reliable for building on. 

**The Grid Imposed: “A Street” and the Engineers**

In 1890, a development company laid out an alphabetical street grid north of Trinity College, with north-south streets running parallel to what is now Buchanan Boulevard.  The first east-west street was called “A Street” - later renamed Markham Avenue.  It ran east down the Trinity Park slope until it hit the Ellerbe ravine near Duke Street, too wet to build across.  The street stopped there and picked back up on the far side, near where 414-416 West Markham still stands today.

The same wet ground cut Washington Street short at Macon, leaving it to resume only at Green Street.  Eventually engineers solved part of the problem by pressing the drainage at Washington and Markham into a brick-and-concrete culvert underground, which allowed the two halves of Markham to be connected.  No such effort was made for Washington.

That gap was not purely an engineering decision.  Markham, with its larger lots and cleaner air, was the address of people who managed the city’s infrastructure rather than operating its machinery.  It connected the prospering Mangum Street corridor to Trinity College, tying the professional class together on one continuous route.  Washington and Macon were left as the industrial fringe, home to Pearl Mill workers and Belt Line laborers.  The soft ground gave the city a convenient excuse.  But the broken street also kept the Strayhorn community isolated on every side - hemmed in by geography the city had decided, quietly and without announcement, not to fix.

**Mangum Street**

While the Washington and Markham intersection sat wet and unresolved, the land to the north and east was still mostly fields and woods - a quiet gap between the Strayhorn settlement, the Pearl Mill village, and the development taking shape along Mangum Street.

That development accelerated in 1901 when a trolley line opened on Mangum, running straight into downtown.  The connection made the street the spine of a viable suburb almost overnight.  Churches, professional offices, and middle-class homes followed.  Attorneys and printers built first, along Mangum and Seeman streets, then the residential grid filled in south to north: Lynch Street, Seeman, Glendale.  By 1930, most lots along those streets were occupied.  As the white middle-class grid pressed northward, the Strayhorn community on Dacian Street found itself increasingly hemmed in, with Washington Street as its hard western boundary and the expanding neighborhood closing in on every other side.  The city had grown around it without ever including it.

**Duke Park**

As Old North Durham filled in, the land further north took shape as Duke Park.  The park itself was established in the early 1930s on land donated by Brodie Duke, with the surrounding streets gradually taking on the Tudor Revival, Colonial Revival, and English Cottage bungalows that define the neighborhood today.  It was a neighborhood built for the automobile era, quiet and residential, a world removed from the mill smoke and dye-stained creek a few blocks south.

**The Human Element: 414 and 416 West Markham**

The house at 416 West Markham went up around 1930.  Its first resident was David B. Weaver, an electrician for the Durham Public Service Company, the firm that had bought the city’s streetcar franchise in 1921 and operated 11 miles of electric track alongside the expanding power grid.  By the time Weaver arrived, the streetcars were already being phased out in favor of buses.  Whether he rode out that transition on the utility side of the business or followed the buses, the records don’t say.  What his daily life probably looked like is easy enough to picture: walk up Markham to Mangum, catch the bus downtown, stop at the Mangum Street Grocery at 1214 North Mangum on the way home.  Lucille Weaver kept the house.  The two of them stayed at 416 for roughly two decades, leaving in the 1950s when the street had long since filled in around them.

The family that moved in after them came from a different world entirely.  The Satterfield name runs through the mid-century records of Durham’s tobacco industry like a thread.  James S. Satterfield had arrived from Flat River Township in Person County in 1907 and worked his way up to become a buyer for Export Leaf Tobacco Company, a subsidiary of British-American Tobacco.  By 1931, he and his brother Frank co-owned both the Liberty Warehouse and the Big Four Warehouse, two of Durham’s major tobacco auction floors where farmers brought their leaf and manufacturers bid on it.  Frank Satterfield’s name was painted on the Liberty Warehouse brick wall in white letters, where it still appears today in a downtown mural.  When the Satterfields moved into 416 West Markham in the 1950s, the cigarette was at the height of its cultural authority and Durham was at the height of its prosperity.  The surgeon general’s report linking smoking to lung cancer was still years away.  The city had not yet been asked to reckon with what it had built itself on.

Next door, 414 West Markham was completed in 1936, a modest cottage built on the last solid ground before the Ellerbe slope.  The Watkins and Davids families were its first residents, though their full story remains in the city directories at Durham County Library, not yet retrieved.  What the construction date alone tells us is enough: 1936 was deep in the Depression, and a house this size, built at that moment, belonged to someone who had saved carefully and moved when they finally could.  Six years after the Weavers broke ground next door, the last lot on this stretch of Markham was finally occupied.

Both houses share a basement problem.  Records for 416 show persistent water intrusion that no repair has fully solved.  The culverted stream beneath the foundation still moves southeast along the same path it always has, indifferent to the concrete poured over it.  Whoever lives at 416 today is living above a running stream.  They may not know it.  The ground has always known.
