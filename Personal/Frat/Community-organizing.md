## Duke Respect Durham / PILOT Context (Frat-1)

Baker's focus on land use extends beyond City Council. Last fall, he helped launch the **Duke Respect Durham** campaign — asks the university to pay $50 million each year to the Bull City. (Yard signs: navy blue with bright yellow letters and image of a bull, lining residential streets around Duke's campus.)

Like many private universities, Duke is a nonprofit and largely exempt from property taxes. The school is also Durham's largest and wealthiest private landowner, with an $11.9 billion endowment and land holdings worth more than $787 million.

Organizers say the town-gown relationship is lopsided. They point to peer institutions like Yale, Princeton and Dartmouth, which make voluntary payments to their home cities each year in lieu of taxes (PILOT agreements).

"It's past time that Duke University strengthened its partnership with the people of Durham, that it does what's right, and that it begins to make reasonable payments to our local governments and to the collective good." — Baker at the group's launch event in September.

"It's a land justice issue, it's a tax justice issue, it's so much wrapped into one thing." — Baker

## Flyer feedback email to Elinor (Frat-3)

**Subject:** Follow-Up on Flyer Feedback

Hi [Elinor],

Thanks for the feedback on the flyer and for sharing the ones from your office!

I spoke with some neighbors, and a few common concerns came up:
1. **Large backyard gatherings** – Sometimes big groups gather to watch sporting events or hold ceremonies. Even when these happen on a Saturday or Sunday afternoon, cheering can go on for hours.
2. **Late-night street noise** – Students lingering in the street while waiting for Ubers can be disruptive late at night.

These issues seem avoidable. Celebrations involving loud cheering could be held indoors, and alternative locations could be chosen for large gatherings.

Neighbors find these moments both amusing and, over time, disruptive. Everyone seemed aware that most of the noise comes from visitors rather than residents.

With Suzy gone, I suspect you may hear fewer complaints about the fraternity — especially since there's a general reluctance to call the police for casual disturbances. However, fewer calls may not always mean improved behavior, so it would be helpful if students could get regular reminders about noise expectations throughout the semester, not just at the start.

Kathy and I would be happy to join any visits.

One question: What's required before students receive a "corrective action" plan (university discipline)? Does it take a certain number of police calls to the address, or do emails to you also count?

Thanks,
Mel & Kathy
