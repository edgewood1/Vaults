# Yes, you are correct. In Aristotle's metaphysics, there are two types…

Yes, you are correct. In Aristotle's metaphysics, there are two types of forms: accidental forms and substantial forms.
Accidental forms, also known as accidental properties or accidents, are the forms that pertain to the attributes or qualities of a substance. These forms are not essential to the identity or nature of the substance but are instead contingent and can vary or be absent without changing the substance itself. Examples of accidental forms include color, shape, size, texture, and other qualities that can change or be altered.
Substantial forms, on the other hand, are the forms that are essential to the identity and nature of a substance. These forms determine the kind of substance something is and are responsible for its characteristic behaviors, functions, and potentialities. Substantial forms give a substance its specific essence or intrinsic nature. For instance, in the case of a living organism like a dog, the substantial form would be the form that makes it a dog and distinguishes it from other types of animals.
So, while accidental forms refer to the attributes or accidental qualities of a substance, substantial forms refer to the essential forms that define the identity and nature of a substance. Both types of forms play a role in Aristotle's metaphysical framework, but they serve different purposes and have different ontological statuses.
