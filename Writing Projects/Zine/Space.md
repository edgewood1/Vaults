You're correct! My previous response oversimplified Edward Feser's view on space and the presence of a medium. He indeed proposes a more nuanced position than simply dismissing space as nothing or an empty void.

Here's a more accurate breakdown of his views:

**Feser does not believe space is an empty void but rather a relational medium with properties.** He criticizes the idea of space as a complete emptiness, arguing that such a notion leads to problems like infinite divisibility and action at a distance.

Instead, he proposes a **relational view of space**, similar to Aristotle and Aquinas, where space arises from the relationships and interactions between material bodies. However, he goes beyond the classical view by attributing certain **properties to this spatial medium**:

* **Potentiality for motion:** Feser argues that space allows for the possibility of material bodies to move, occupy positions, and exert forces upon each other. This potentiality for motion is inherent in the nature of space itself.
* **Structure and order:** He contends that the interactions between bodies and their motions influence the structure and order of space. This influence isn't a one-way street; the structure of space also affects how bodies move and behave.
* **Causality and change:** Space, according to Feser, isn't static but can be affected by the dynamics of moving bodies. He suggests that changes in the motions and configurations of bodies can cause corresponding changes in the spatial medium itself.

**However, it's important to differentiate Feser's "medium" from how it might be conceived in physics.** He emphasizes that his idea isn't meant to be identical to scientific models of fields or forces like the electromagnetic field. His primary focus is on the philosophical and metaphysical implications of space, not necessarily proposing a specific physical mechanism.
