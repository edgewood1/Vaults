# Molar/summary

 <span style="font-family: .SFUI-Bold; font-size: 31.0;">
     **Molar/summary

In Deleuze and Guattari's philosophy, the**
 </span> **molar** is not merely a mode of signification; it is a level of organization characterized by stable, identifiable, and representable entities (aggregates), often associated with social control and established power structures. Signification and representation are functions often tied to the molar sphere, but the molar is a broader ontological and political category. 

## **Molar vs. Molecular**
The distinction between molar and molecular is central to their work, particularly in *A Thousand Plateaus*. They are not distinguished by size (macro vs. micro), but by their nature, organization, and function. 
* **Molar:** This realm consists of "coded wholes" and "assemblages" that are highly organized, easily perceived, and clearly demarcated.
	* **Characteristics:** It involves fixed identities, binary oppositions, and hierarchical structures. Examples include gender identity (man/woman), personhood, family units (Oedipus complex critique), and the apparatuses of the State.
	* **Relation to Signification:** The molar level is where stable, representational meaning (signification) typically operates. It functions through clear, segmenting lines and established codes that order social reality.
	* **Ontology:** It is associated with "being" and a fixed self-identity.
* **Molecular:** This realm is composed of "decoded flows" and "microphysics of desire".
	* **Characteristics:** It is a domain of imperceptible movements, continuous variation, affects, and "becoming". It is "smooth" rather than "striated" (organized) space. Examples include the rhizome, packs, and singular events.
	* **Relation to Signification:** The molecular often escapes traditional signification and representation; it deals with "affects" which "represent nothing". 

## **The Role of the Molar**
The molar level, through its emphasis on stable identity and representation, is often seen by Deleuze and Guattari as a force of *stasis* and *control*, which "deprives desire of its objective being" by forcing it into predefined channels (like the Freudian lack or Oedipal structure). They advocate for a "becoming-molecular" to break down these rigid molar aggregates and explore new possibilities for existence and social organization. 
In essence, while the molar is the domain where signification has its clearest, most stable function, Deleuze and Guattari view it as a limited, organizing, and often repressive mode of existence, not merely a neutral linguistic mechanism. They critique representational logic in favor of a focus on the *forces* and *flows* of the molecular.

Your understanding is a good starting point: the molar involves rigid ideas and fixed associations. Beyond this cognitive aspect, the molar refers to large-scale **physical, social, and political organizations** that are clearly structured, observable, and impose stable, identifiable functions and identities. 
Here are some concrete examples of the molar:

## **1. Social Identities**
The concept of fixed, categorized identities is a primary molar example. These identities are "punctual" and clearly demarcated, often operating through binary oppositions (e.g., "normal/abnormal"). 
* **Gender:** "Man" and "Woman" as stable, socially defined categories are molar entities. These identities come with specific expected roles and behaviors that help regulate social order.
* **The Self/Personality:** Your sense of a unified, consistent "self" or "personality" is a molar construction. Deleuze and Guattari argue this seemingly stable identity is built upon underlying, less visible molecular processes (conflicts, desires, crises).
* **Race, Class, and Nationality:** These are all molar categories that classify, organize, and control populations, imposing functions and norms upon individuals. 

## **2. Institutions and Structures**
Any large-scale, organized system that imposes order and follows rigid rules falls under the molar category. 
* **The State:** The government, legal structures, and bureaucracies are the molar apparatus *par excellence*. They operate through rigid segmentation, laws, and the power to define who belongs and who does not.
* **The Family Unit:** The traditional nuclear family, with its defined roles (mother, father, child), is a molar aggregate that serves to channel desire and regulate social reproduction.
* **Capitalism:** The global economic system, with its defined classes and market mechanisms, functions as a highly organized molar machine that assigns specific economic functions (e.g., "consumer," "worker"). 

## **3. Physical Objects and Organization**
The molar isn't limited to social concepts; it applies to physical organization as well.
* **A Building:** A house or office building is a molar assemblage—a bounded, clearly structured entity with a defined purpose (dwelling, work). The materials are selected and arranged to express a specific, stable function.
* **A Straight Line/Map:** A road on a map is a rigid, striated (molar) line that dictates movement, in contrast to a desire path or animal trail, which might be a more molecular, "smooth" line of flight.
* **A "Pack" with a Leader:** A gang with a clearly defined, centralized leader is a molar organization, even if small in scale, because its structure is hierarchical and organized. 
The key takeaway is that molar entities are defined by their **organization, stability, identity, and representational legibility**. They are what is *perceptible* and *coded* in the world, serving to order chaotic flows into manageable, functional forms.

That is correct. The molar is much more than just a way of seeing or a cognitive framework; it is fundamentally an **ontological force of organization** and a **way of arranging ourselves and things** in the material world.
It functions as a structural principle that shapes reality itself, making the world legible, stable, and controllable.
Here’s a breakdown of the molar as arrangement and force:

## **1. The Molar as Arrangement/Organization**
The molar level is where "striation" occurs. It involves segmenting space, time, and bodies into discrete units with fixed functions.
* **Arrangement of Bodies:** Society arranges human bodies into categories (male/female, citizen/immigrant, employed/unemployed) and spatial organizations (workplace, home, prison). These arrangements are physical realities, not just mental constructs.
* **Arrangement of Territory:** The molar operates through borders, property lines, and urban planning—physical divisions that determine where flows of people, goods, and energy are allowed to go.
* **A Way of Functioning:** It is the operational mode of social machines that "overcode" (impose a code upon) desire and potential. The factory floor, for instance, arranges people and machines into a highly efficient, molar assembly line.

## **2. The Molar as a "Force" or Assemblage**
Deleuze and Guattari describe the molar as a powerful *assemblage* that exerts force. It is not an abstract concept but a concrete machine of social production.
* **A Stabilizing Force:** The molar resists change and flux. It is a force of *conservation*and *stasis*, aiming to maintain equilibrium and established order. It seeks to capture the free-flowing (molecular) energy of desire and channel it into productive, predictable systems (e.g., channeling raw desire into the structured pursuit of money or power).
* **A "War Machine" of the State:** In a political sense, the molar is the force of centralized power, the State apparatus that defines the norms of existence and suppresses deviation. It *imposes* its arrangements through laws, policing, and normative social pressures.

## **Summary of the Distinction**

| Feature                                 | Molar                                   | Molecular                               |
|-----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Nature                                  | Organized, structured, aggregate        | Flowing, fluctuating, singularities     |
| Function                                | Stability, control, representation      | Variation, escape, affect               |
| Ontology                                | Being (fixed identity)                  | Becoming (continuous change)            |
| Mode                                    | Arrangement, Striation, Force of Stasis | Force of Experimentation, Flight        |

The key takeaway is that the molar is a very real, material force that structures our environment, our social reality, and our perceived identities, providing the "heavy, rigid segments" of life that we must navigate, disrupt, or utilize
