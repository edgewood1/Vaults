Object

In object-oriented philosophy, an idea or a property is considered an object in its own right because the term "object" has a different meaning than it does in classical philosophy. This is a fundamental difference in metaphysics.
Classical vs. Object-Oriented Philosophy
In classical philosophy (like that of Aquinas), the world is organized around primary substances. A primary substance is an independent, foundational reality, like a tree or a person. Ideas and properties (qualities or accidents) are not objects in their own right because they cannot exist without a substance to be in. The color green can't exist without a green thing. The mind's concept of a tree can't exist without a mind to have it.
In object-oriented philosophy (OOP), the term "object" is a universal category that applies to anything that exists, regardless of whether it's independent. In this view, a physical object, a property, a relationship, and an idea are all considered objects. An idea is an "immaterial object," but it is an object nonetheless, with a reality of its own.
The two views differ on the concept of independence. Classical philosophy is a substance-based metaphysics, where the foundational reality is a hierarchy with independent substances at the top. OOP is an object-based metaphysics, where everything is an object in a flat hierarchy. It intentionally dissolves the classical distinction between a thing and its properties.


**Chapter 1 — Being, Form, and the Integrity of Things**

Being is the foundation of all that is—the ultimate act of awareness and intellect. Everything that exists participates in Being, and every creature, no matter how simple or complex, reflects a degree of this fundamental reality.

A primary substance is a concrete being that exists in itself, not merely as a collection of parts or qualities. A tree, for example, is not just a heap of wood, sap, and cells; it is one unified being—a whole that remains itself through constant material change.

When we ask what something is, we enter the realm of causes—not simply causes in the sense of physical triggers, but deeper principles that explain existence and identity. We can distinguish four such causes: material, efficient, formal, and final.

These causes can be paired. The material cause is what something is made of—the matter it is composed of. The efficient cause refers to that which brings the thing into being or sustains it. For the tree, this is the surrounding physical substances: sunlight, air, water, and the interplay of natural forces that move and transform the matter. These two belong to the physical order: they describe what acts and what is acted upon.

Yet the tree is more than its chemistry or its external movers. It possesses an intelligible, metaphysical "presence" that gives it identity and purpose. This is its Substantial Form. This form is the internal principle that organizes the tree’s matter in a particular shape in order to function in a particular way (its formal cause) and reach a particular end (its final cause).

The Form is not a hidden part of the tree, nor a separate ghost within it, but the living, unifying presence that gives coherence and structure to the matter. It holds the many parts together as one and directs their activity toward the flourishing of the whole.

The Form’s activity is most visible in the way a living being sustains and repairs itself. When the tree draws nutrients from the soil or heals a broken branch, what we witness is not mere physical reaction but the matter’s obedience to its form—its inner necessity to act in accordance with what it is. This necessity is not a conscious decision but an automatic, non-intelligent directive; it is an inherent, automatic tendency written into the fabric of its being. The Form governs the behavior of the matter from within, just as a magnetic field  silently orders a cloud of iron filings into a unified pattern. The filings do not “know” the pattern; they simply fall into order under its invisible influence.

Consider the example of human skin exposed to sunlight. The energy from ultraviolet light is an efficient cause, acting upon the material cause (the skin’s cells). If left at that, the result might be mere destruction. Yet the skin does not simply disintegrate. Its Substantial Form—the organizing principle that makes it living tissue—compels the matter to respond in a way that preserves the unity of the organism. It initiates healing rather than dissolution. The Form thus expresses itself as an inward striving toward its final cause—the integrity of the body.

So, while the physical world provides material and efficient causes (the stuff and the movers), the intelligible and purposive side of reality is revealed in form and finality.

Matter alone cannot organize itself. Even DNA, the intricate molecule that carries the genetic code, is still matter. What determines that these atoms should constitute DNA and not some other compound? The molecule’s structure presupposes a principle of intelligible order. The question “what organizes matter into this form?” cannot be answered by appealing to more matter, any more than the meaning of a word can be explained by the ink that forms its letters.

We may trace a tree’s ancestry (its efficient causes) through countless generations, but this only pushes the mystery back. The more fundamental question remains: what allows this tree to be here and now, rather than nothing at all? The answer lies in Being itself, expressed through the tree’s Form—the imprint of divine intelligence that makes it a living unity at this very moment.

Our perception, however, lags behind this reality. What we perceive are merely the accidental forms—the changing features, colors, and shapes by which the substance manifests itself. These qualities are how we know the thing, but they are not what the thing is. Deeper still is the substantial form—the organizing reality that makes the thing what it is. And finally, beneath all things, is Being—the pure act that makes possible any existence at all.

To grasp something truly, then, is to see through its appearances to the intelligible principle that holds it together — to understand the unity that endures amid change. The Substantial Form is the bridge between matter and Being, between the visible and the intelligible, between what merely happens and what *is*.
