# Fisher


Mark Fisher defines <u>**[capitalist realism](https://www.google.com/search?q=capitalist+realism&ie=UTF-8&oe=UTF-8&hl=en-us&client=safari&mstk=AUtExfA3mDrnGiPxyBltbmus45IXL8tvsGUwhJdMfnxT1IP6U3i3IJexBAyuej5II7E4RVPO9LQ00QEKTEX9g1su5AEw9GENiJ5OnzsBg030DgMDJaB_tbNAeYZZ1ZBp2j7nNh4&csui=3&ved=2ahUKEwjav5DQ7_aRAxXO6BoGHfPUEmoQgK4QegQIARAB)**</u> as the pervasive atmosphere and widespread belief that capitalism is the only viable political and economic system, making it impossible to even imagine coherent alternatives, thus functioning as an invisible barrier to societal change and limiting thought and action. It's characterized by the internalization of capitalist logic into all aspects of life, from work and education to personal identity and mental health, creating a sense that problems are individual failings rather than systemic issues. 
**Key Aspects of Capitalist Realism:**
* **Impossibility of Alternatives:** The core idea is the widespread feeling that "it's easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism".
* **Pervasive Atmosphere:** It's a cultural and psychological condition that shapes everything, making capitalist assumptions seem like common sense.
* **Internalization of Problems:** It shifts blame for failures (like poverty or mental illness) onto individuals, ignoring systemic causes, such as viewing depression solely as a chemical imbalance rather than a response to systemic pressures.
* **Business Ontology:** It imposes a "business logic" onto all areas, including healthcare and education, treating them as businesses to be run efficiently.
* **Stifled Imagination:** It dulls the ability to envision different futures, leading to cultural stasis and a focus on superficial desires rather than profound change.
* <u>**[Workplace](https://www.google.com/search?q=Workplace&ie=UTF-8&oe=UTF-8&hl=en-us&client=safari&mstk=AUtExfA3mDrnGiPxyBltbmus45IXL8tvsGUwhJdMfnxT1IP6U3i3IJexBAyuej5II7E4RVPO9LQ00QEKTEX9g1su5AEw9GENiJ5OnzsBg030DgMDJaB_tbNAeYZZ1ZBp2j7nNh4&csui=3&ved=2ahUKEwjav5DQ7_aRAxXO6BoGHfPUEmoQgK4QegQIAxAG)**</u> **& Education:** It normalizes precarious work, constant evaluation, and self-surveillance, turning even leisure and free time into extensions of work. 
In essence, Fisher argues that capitalist realism is a powerful ideology that makes capitalism seem natural and inevitable, even as it produces widespread alienation and crisis, preventing us from collectively imagining and building a better world
Yes, for Mark Fisher, **capitalist realism** is an ideological point of view that functions like a "pervasive atmosphere," shaping what we consider to be "realistic" and "possible" in politics, economics, and culture. 
Fisher absolutely believed that **alternatives are possible**, though he argued they are currently being blocked by our inability to imagine them. His work was dedicated to finding "cracks" in this realism to prove that capitalism is not as seamless as it appears. 

# **Fisher’s Path to an Alternative**
Fisher’s search for an alternative moved through several key concepts:
* **Identifying Systemic Weaknesses:** He argued that areas like the **mental health crisis**, **environmental collapse**, and **mass bureaucracy** (which he called "Market Stalinism") are points where capitalism clearly fails to be "realistic" or functional. Exposing these failures is the first step toward breaking the spell.
* **Acid Communism:** In his later, unfinished work, Fisher proposed "Acid Communism" as a way to reclaim the radical social imagination of the 1960s and 70s counterculture. This was not about drugs, but about an "aestheticization of everyday life" and the belief that the world is malleable and can be reorganized around collective joy and freedom.
* **Postcapitalist Desire:** Fisher believed the left must stop being just "anti-capitalist" and instead focus on **postcapitalist desire**—showing that life after capitalism would actually be more pleasurable, creative, and autonomous than the current system.
* **Collective Agency:** He stressed that while an alternative is currently "unimaginable," it can be built through **collective endeavor** and political organization rather than individual effort. 
Ultimately, Fisher’s project was to move from a state where "nothing can happen" to one where "suddenly anything is possible again" by proving that the current system is an unstable construction, not a natural law. 
Building an alternative to capitalist realism requires moving from individual despair to collective action and reclaiming the "social imagination." In 2026, several concrete movements and models have emerged that challenge the notion that "there is no alternative" (TINA).

## **1. Polticization of Mental Health**
Fisher argued that capitalist realism "privatizes" stress, treating anxiety and depression as individual chemical imbalances rather than results of systemic pressure. 
* **Anti-Raids and Care Collectives:** Modern grassroots movements focus on "collective care" rather than just individual self-care, linking mental well-being to political security.
* **Consciousness Raising:** Drawing from 1970s feminist techniques, contemporary groups use "consciousness-raising" to turn private misery into a "subjugated group consciousness," creating a shared "we" that can struggle for change. 

## **2. Reclaiming the Commons**
Alternatives are being built by creating "parallel economies" that operate outside of standard market logic. 
* **Platform Cooperatives:** Worker-owned digital platforms that challenge corporate giants like Uber or Amazon by prioritizing democratic decision-making and equitable profit sharing.
* **Community Land Trusts & Energy Democracy:** Projects such as **Renewable Energy Commons** and community-led solar generation (e.g., the *Power Shift* coalition in Mauritius) reclaim essential resources as shared public goods rather than private commodities.
* **Open-Source Movements:** The continuous growth of open-source software and hardware designs provides a model for innovation without proprietary restrictions or profit-driven gatekeeping. 

## **3. Acid Communism and Cultural Re-enchantment**
Fisher's unfinished concept of **Acid Communism** focuses on reclaiming the radical potential of the 1960s to imagine a world centered on collective joy. 
* **Meme Culture and Theory Vlogging:** Online communities utilize "theory vlogging" and meme culture to break down the "pervasive atmosphere" of capitalist realism, making complex anti-capitalist ideas accessible and aesthetically appealing.
* **Postcapitalist Desire:** Art and social movements that emphasize that life after capitalism would be more pleasurable and creative—not just more ethical—help counter the "depressive hedonia" of the current system. 

## **4. New Economic Blueprints**
Concrete policy experiments in 2026 are testing ways to decouple human survival from wage labor. 
* **Universal Basic Income (UBI) & Infrastructure (UBI 2.0):** Beyond simple cash transfers, "Universal Basic Infrastructure" models propose providing free access to housing, healthcare, education, and clean energy as a baseline right.
* **AI Sovereignty:** As of 2026, nations and local organizations are pursuing "AI sovereignty" to ensure that large-scale data and automation tools are managed as public infrastructure rather than private monopolies.
* **Participatory Budgeting:** Local governments (inspired by models like Porto Alegre) increasingly use direct democratic processes to allow citizens to determine how public funds are spent. 
Fisher's work addresses the relationship between capitalism and social disorder, though he frames "hedonism" and "crime" differently than traditional law-and-order perspectives.

## **1. Hedonism as a Symptom, Not a Cause**
Fisher distinguishes between "depressive hedonia"—the numbing, repetitive pleasure-seeking found under capitalism (like mindless scrolling or binge-watching)—and a radical, collective "Acid Communism". 
* **Current "Hedonistic Crimes":**Fisher argues that much of the "generalized criminality" seen today (such as the violence in gangster movies or hip-hop) is a direct reflection of capitalism's "dog-eat-dog" reality.
* **The System's Logic:** In his view, capitalism *is* the most "naked, shameless, direct, brutal exploitation," and what we call "crime" often mirrors the system's own "egotistical calculation". 

## **2. Redefining Crime and Law**
In a post-capitalist system, the need for traditional policing would likely shift because the *categories*of crime would change: 
* **Abolition of Property Crimes:**Since the primary role of modern policing is the protection of private property, Fisher and related theorists argue that in a society based on "commoning," property crimes (theft, burglary, fraud) would largely disappear as social causes are removed.
* **Ending the "Privatization of Stress":** By treating mental health as a social rather than individual issue, a new system would address the root causes of antisocial behavior—stress, fear, and inequality—that often lead to what are currently viewed as "hedonistic" outbursts. 

## **3. Policing vs. Social Management**
While Fisher did not explicitly detail a police-less state, his theories align with broader post-capitalist critiques of "Market Stalinism"—the massive, ineffective bureaucracy of surveillance and targets that characterizes current governance. 
* **Collective Agency:** He believed in "collective endeavor" and "universal intelligence" to run society, suggesting that social order would be maintained through community accountability and robust public infrastructure rather than the "techno-surveillance" and "ruthless work environments" of the present.
* **Acid Criminology:** Newer academic interpretations of his work, termed "Acid Criminology," suggest that instead of repressive criminal justice policies, society should focus on "denaturalizing" the existing order and collaborating with social movements to affect transformative change. 

## **4. The Challenge of "Human Nature"**
A central pillar of **capitalist realism**is the claim that capitalism is the *only* system compatible with "human nature" and its innate selfish desires. Fisher argues that this is a myth used to justify the current order. He suggests that once "necessities of life are taken care of," human desire can be decoupled from economic accumulation and redirected toward art, culture, and collective well-being. 
In Mark Fisher’s framework and broader post-capitalist theory as of 2026, the question of home security and "crime" is addressed by redefining the relationship between people, space, and the state. 
The problem you describe is often cited by Fisher as a hallmark of **capitalist realism**: the inability to imagine safety or order without the specific legal and punitive structures of capitalism. 

## **1. Personal Property vs. Private Property**
Post-capitalist theorists distinguish between **private property**(factories, land for rent, means of production) and **personal property**(your home, clothes, toothbrush). 
* **Abolishing Private Property:**This refers to ending the system where individuals own resources to extract profit from others (like landlords).
* **Protecting Personal Space:** Your home remains your "personal space" based on **stewardship**—the right to use and care for a space—rather than a legal title for financial speculation. 

## **2. Redefining Crime and its Motives**
Fisher argues that "hedonistic" or violent crimes are often symptoms of a society that prioritizes individual competition and "naked exploitation". 
* **Removing Root Causes:**Abolitionist perspectives in 2026 argue that most home invasions and property destruction are driven by **poverty, addiction, and mental health crises**. By meeting everyone's basic needs (housing, food, and healthcare), the systemic pressure to commit these crimes is radically reduced.
* **The "Social Contract":** In a system where resources like housing are guaranteed for all, "destroying a home" takes on a different moral character, as there is no financial profit to be gained and everyone already has their own secure space. 

## **3. Community Safety vs. State Policing**
The alternative to "policing" isn't necessarily "no protection," but rather **community-led safety**. 
* **Collective Accountability:**Instead of a distant state force, communities use models like **transformative justice** and community responder units to handle harm and conflict.
* **Social Defense:** Security is viewed as a collective responsibility rather than a specialized service. This involves building well-resourced communities where neighbors are empowered to intervene and support each other, rendering traditional police "obsolete".
* **Rehabilitative Justice:** For individuals who continue to cause harm, the focus shifts from punitive "cages" to intensive rehabilitation and addressing the psychological or social causes of their behavior. 

## **4. The Challenge of "Human Nature"**
Critics of this view argue that without law, society reverts to "survival of the fittest". Fisher’s response is that our current "dog-eat-dog" view of human nature is an **ideological fantasy** taught by capitalism to make its own brutality seem natural. He suggests that human desires can be redirected toward collective joy and social connection once the artificial scarcity of capitalism is removed
