Here are some American authors who wrote about life in Appalachia in the late 1800s and early 1900s:

* **Sherwood Anderson (1876-1941):** Anderson's most famous work, *Winesburg, Ohio* (1919), is a collection of interrelated short stories that explore the lives of people in a small Midwestern town. While not strictly set in Appalachia, the themes of isolation, loneliness, and the search for meaning resonate with the Appalachian experience.
[Image of Sherwood Anderson (1876-1941)]
* **Mary Noailles Murfree (1850-1922):** Nicknamed "Charles Egbert Craddock," Murfree was a prominent writer of local color fiction who focused on the customs, dialect, and folklore of the Tennessee mountains. Her novel *The Prophet of the Great Smoky Mountains* (1885) is a classic of Appalachian literature.
[Image of Mary Noailles Murfree (1850-1922)]
* **Olive Tilford Dargan (1870-1938):** Dargan's novel *A Borderland Tale* (1906) explores the lives of people living on the Kentucky-Tennessee border during the Civil War. Her work is known for its vivid descriptions of the Appalachian landscape and its sympathetic portrayal of mountain characters.

* **James Still (1906-1983):** Still is best known for his novel *River of Earth* (1940), which tells the story of a tenant farming family in Kentucky. His work is notable for its use of Appalachian dialect and its focus on the social and economic challenges faced by mountain communities.

* **Jesse Stuart (1907-1984):** Stuart was a prolific writer who wrote over 80 books about life in the Kentucky mountains. His work is often sentimental and nostalgic, but it also provides valuable insights into Appalachian culture and history.
[Image of Jesse Stuart (1907-1984)]

These are just a few of the many American authors who have written about life in Appalachia. Their work provides a rich and complex portrait of a region that has long been misunderstood and marginalized.

Here are some prominent non-fiction writers who explored life in Appalachia during the late 1800s and early 1900s:

* **Horace Kephart (1862-1931):** Kephart was a writer and conservationist who is best known for his book *Our Southern Highlanders* (1913). This work documented his experiences living among the people of the Great Smoky Mountains and offered insights into their customs, traditions, and challenges. 
[Image of Horace Kephart (1862-1931)] 

* **Celia Furth (1865-1951):** Furth was a social worker and photographer who documented the lives of coal miners and their families in Appalachia. Her photographs, many of which were published in her book *Children of the Poor* (1904), provide a powerful and poignant record of the harsh realities faced by many Appalachian residents in the early 20th century.


* **John C. Campbell (1867-1914):** Campbell was a folklorist who collected and published Appalachian ballads, songs, and stories. His work, including *The Southern Appalachian Folk-Lore Society* (1915), helped to preserve the rich cultural heritage of the region.


* **Dorothy Scarborough (1878-1935):**  Scarborough was a folklorist and writer who collected and published a vast amount of material on Appalachian culture, including folktales, music, and customs. Her work, such as *A Song Catcher in Southern Mountains* (1937), is a valuable resource for understanding Appalachian traditions.
[Image of Dorothy Scarborough (1878-1935)]

These non-fiction writers, along with the fiction authors mentioned previously, offer a multifaceted perspective on life in Appalachia during this period. Their work sheds light on the region's unique culture, the challenges faced by its residents, and the beauty and resilience of its people.
